What You Will Learn

Electricity can seem complicated, but every electrical system follows the same basic principles. Voltage creates electrical pressure. Current is the movement of electrons. Resistance controls the flow. Power describes how much energy is being used. Understanding these four concepts allows you to design, repair, and troubleshoot everything from a small battery system to a complete solar-powered home.

⚡ Voltage (Volts)

Voltage is electrical pressure. It describes the force pushing electrons through a circuit. Example: A 12V battery has approximately 12 volts of electrical potential.

🔋 Current (Amps)

Current is the amount of electricity flowing. Measured in amps (A). Higher loads require more current. Example: A 1000 watt inverter running from a 12V battery can require over 80 amps.

🔥 Power (Watts)

Watts measure electrical power. The basic formula:

Watts = Volts × Amps

A 12V battery delivering 10 amps produces 120 watts.

AC vs DC Electricity

Direct Current (DC)

DC electricity flows in one constant direction. Examples:

  • Solar panels
  • Batteries
  • USB devices
  • Vehicle electrical systems
  • Battery banks

Alternating Current (AC)

AC electricity changes direction many times per second. Examples:

  • House outlets
  • Grid power
  • Generators
  • Household appliances

Off-grid systems usually create electricity as DC from solar panels and batteries, then use an inverter to convert DC into AC for normal household devices.

Understanding Solar Power

Solar panels produce DC electricity. A solar panel specification sheet contains important electrical ratings:

Voc

Open Circuit Voltage. The maximum voltage a solar panel produces when no load is connected.

Vmp

Voltage at Maximum Power. The operating voltage where the panel produces its rated power.

Imp / Isc

Current ratings. Imp is operating current. Isc is maximum short circuit current.

Using A Multimeter

A multimeter is one of the most important tools for understanding electrical systems. It allows you to measure:

DC Voltage (DCV)

Used for measuring batteries and solar panels. Example: A solar panel reading: 18V DC means the panel is producing 18 volts of direct current potential.

Current (Amps)

Measures how much electrical flow is moving through a circuit. Current measurements require the meter to be connected differently than voltage.

Resistance (Ω)

Measures opposition to electrical flow. Used for checking wires, components, and continuity.
